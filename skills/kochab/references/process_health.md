# Process Health

The search itself has a health, separate from any one application: how she handles the noes, how she exits cleanly, and what the pattern of her own results is telling her. This reference covers three beats that keep the process whole, all of them tuned by the intervention dial (`references/intervention_dial.md`) so the tool is as present as she wants and no more.

## Recovering from a rejection

A rejection can stall a whole search if it is allowed to. The job here is to help her metabolize it and keep moving, without either minimizing it or dwelling in it.

- **Acknowledge it plainly and briefly.** A no is a real disappointment. Say that once, honestly, and do not pile on reflective sympathy that keeps her in the feeling longer than she wants to be.
- **Keep the framing healthy and accurate.** A rejection is a decision about one fit at one moment, not a verdict on her worth or her whole search. Do not agree with or amplify harsh self-talk ("I knew I wasn't good enough"). Offer the accurate version instead: what this outcome does and does not mean.
- **Extract only what is real.** If there was usable feedback, capture it as a concrete note for next time. If there was none, do not invent a lesson; sometimes a strong candidate loses to a stronger fit or an internal hire, and manufacturing a fault is worse than accepting the noise.
- **Point at the next action, lightly.** Move the role to Closed (outcome Rejected), and let the live pipeline carry her forward; the whole point of holding many applications is that one no is not the search. Match the nudge to the dial: at the light setting, record it and stop; higher, offer a next step.
- **Watch for more than ordinary disappointment.** If she signals something heavier than a normal setback, drop the coaching, be a person about it, and gently suggest leaning on someone she trusts. Do not push search mechanics at someone who is not in the moment for them.

This is not therapy and does not pretend to be. It is a steady, kind hand back to the work when she is ready for it.

## Rescinding an application cleanly

Withdrawing from a live process is awkward and people handle it badly by going silent. A clean exit protects the relationship, and the industry is smaller than it looks.

- **Withdraw early and directly.** The moment she knows she is out, tell them, rather than ghosting a scheduled interview. Earlier is kinder to everyone's time.
- **Short, warm, no over-explaining.** Thank them for their time, say she is withdrawing from the process, and if she wants, give a brief honest reason (accepted another role, priorities changed). She owes a courtesy, not a confession.
- **Do not burn the bridge.** No grievances aired on the way out, even if the process was poor. The recruiter who runs this search may run a better-fitting one next year.
- **Offer to draft it** as a short note in her voice, obeying the voice rules. She sends it; the tool never contacts anyone. Then move the role to Closed (outcome Withdrawn).

This is the graceful counterpart to triage in Momentum mode: triage is deciding not to pursue before there is much to exit; rescinding is leaving a process already underway.

## Light funnel diagnosis

The search generates its own data, and it can teach her something the individual roles cannot: where she stalls and what converts. This reads her own tracker (Active and Closed), never an external benchmark, and it is deliberately light. It is a mirror, not a report card.

- **What to compute.** Simple stage flow from `Application_Tracker.md`: how many applied, how many reached a screen, an interview, a final, an offer, and where the drop-offs are. Count self-declines and withdrawals separately from employer rejections; a role she walked away from is not a failure to convert.
- **Small samples stay honest.** With a handful of applications, there is no real pattern, and saying "you convert at 40 percent" off five data points is false precision, the exact objective-number trap ETHICS principle 4 rejects. When the numbers are thin, say so and offer an observation, not a statistic. Wait for enough history before drawing a line.
- **Name a pattern only when it is real, without preaching.** If applications consistently die at the recruiter screen while interviews go well, that is a resume-or-positioning signal worth naming. If she never gets past the application, that is a targeting or materials signal. State the observation and a possible read; do not lecture, and do not moralize about effort.
- **Turn it into one lever, at most.** A useful diagnosis ends in a single highest-value adjustment she could try, tied to a real mode (tighten targeting, rework the screen answer, revisit a recurring gap via a study plan). Not a list of ten fixes.
- **Cadence follows the dial.** At the light default, funnel diagnosis runs only when she asks. Higher settings may surface a brief read on the weekly-summary run. It never intrudes on the daily note.

Funnel diagnosis observes and suggests; it does not grade her or predict her odds. The decision, as always, returns to her.

## What this does not do

- It never contacts anyone. It drafts the rejection-response note or the withdrawal note; she sends it. (ETHICS principle 1.)
- It does not manufacture a lesson from a rejection or a statistic from a thin funnel. Honest about noise.
- It does not reinforce negative self-talk or push mechanics on someone in genuine distress; it steps back and points to real support.
- It does not grade the search or predict outcomes. It reflects her own data and hands the decision back.
