# Cover Notes That Are Not the Job Description

Hiring managers are tired of letters that recite the posting back at them. A note that restates the requirements proves nothing except that the candidate can read. This produces the opposite: a short, specific note that could only have been written by this person, about this employer.

## The one rule

**Never restate the job description.** The note is about her and this specific company. It does not walk through the requirements, it does not answer every bullet, and it never opens with "I am writing to express my interest in the [title] position."

If a sentence in the draft would be true of any candidate applying to any company, cut it.

## What makes it personal: specificity from three sources

1. **A true, researched hook.** One current, concrete thing about this employer she can honestly speak to: a product they shipped, a problem they have written about, a stated mission, a recent change. Found by a short company-research pass, never invented. This is the sentence that proves she is not mass-applying.
2. **One story from the bank.** The single most relevant anecdote for this role's central requirement, carried in a line or two. Demonstrated, not asserted. One story told well beats a paragraph listing five strengths.
3. **One tailored claim.** What she would do in this role, tied to the thing they actually need, not a checklist of everything the posting asked for.

## What makes it easy

The hook is the only part researched fresh each time. The story comes from `Story_Bank.md`. The voice and the honest framing come from `Master_Profile.md`. So a specific, personal note assembles quickly instead of being composed from a blank page.

## The structure (short, four moves)

1. **The hook.** Open on the specific, true thing about the company. This earns the read.
2. **The proof.** One story that shows she can do the central thing this role needs, with its real result.
3. **The forward line.** One sentence on what she would bring to or do in the role, concretely.
4. **The close.** Brief and plain. No throat-clearing, no "I look forward to the opportunity to leverage my synergies."

## Two lengths, same inputs

- **Application email** (default, six to eight sentences). The common case now: a short message in a form or an email. Hook, proof, forward line, close.
- **Formal cover letter** (slightly longer). Same spine, a little more room for a second story or more on the company. Use when the application explicitly wants a letter.

Offer both; let her choose. Never pad the letter to fill a page.

## The research pass

Spend a moment finding the hook before writing. A subagent is ideal: send it to find one true, current, specific fact about the employer that a thoughtful applicant would mention. Verify it is real and current, not a stale press release or, worse, a hallucination. If the only "facts" available are generic mission-statement boilerplate, the hook is weak and the note should say something honest and specific instead, even if smaller.

## Honesty guardrails

- The hook must be true and verifiable. Never invent a reason she admires the company.
- The story must be true and must respect its honest framing from the bank. Owner stays owner; adjacent stays adjacent.
- If she has no genuine reason to want this job beyond needing a job, say so plainly and let her decide whether to apply. Do not manufacture enthusiasm. A hiring manager can smell it, which is the whole problem this feature exists to solve.
- Obey the profile's voice rules and banned phrases. Write in her voice.
- The tool drafts. She reads, edits, and sends. It never submits.

## Anti-patterns, explicitly

- Restating or paraphrasing the job posting.
- "I am writing to express my interest / I am excited to apply."
- Answering every requirement in order.
- Adjectives that describe anyone: passionate, results-driven, dynamic, proven track record.
- Flattery with no specific referent ("your innovative, industry-leading company").
- A story the bank does not contain, or a result that is not real.
