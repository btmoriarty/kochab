# Interview Prep

Prepare her for a specific interview, grounded in her profile, her story bank, and the actual job description. Not generic advice. The value is the mapping: which of her real stories answers which likely question, and where she is thin and needs a plan.

## Before drafting, get the specifics

- **Which role, which company, which stage.** A recruiter screen, a hiring-manager call, a panel, or an onsite are different interviews. Ask which one, and how many rounds she knows about.
- **Format if known.** Phone, video, a battery of one-on-ones over a few hours, a full onsite, a presentation or work sample.
- **Anything she was told.** Interviewer names, a stated focus ("this round is system design," "this is a values fit"), a take-home.

If she does not know the format, prepare for the most likely next stage and say so.

## What to produce

### Likely questions, tied to her stories
Pull the questions this role and level actually tend to ask, from the job description and the function, split into:

- **Role and domain questions** the posting implies.
- **Behavioral questions** the level implies.

For each likely question, name the **story from `Story_Bank.md`** that answers it best, and how to frame it for this employer. This is the core of the prep: she should walk in knowing which real anecdote lands where. If a likely question has no story to answer it, that is a gap to flag, not a blank to bluff.

### Her own questions to ask
Three to five specific, non-generic questions for her to ask them, drawn from the company research and the role. Questions that show she understood the job, not "what is the culture like."

### The thin spots
Where the job description asks for something her profile is light on, name it and give her a way to handle the question honestly: what to acknowledge, what adjacent experience to pivot to, and what not to overclaim. The honesty guardrails apply here exactly as they do on the resume. Better she has a true, composed answer ready than gets surprised into a bluff.

### Logistics for the format
Short, practical notes for the specific format: timing, what to have in front of her, how to handle a panel versus a single interviewer, how to manage an all-day onsite.

## Prep by format

The mapping above is the same at every stage; what changes is emphasis and logistics. Prepare for the format she is actually facing. If she does not know which, prepare the most likely next stage and say so. The stages line up with the pipeline Stage vocabulary and the phase map in `references/stage_guidance.md`.

### Recruiter screen
The cheapest filter, built to cut on avoidable things. Weight the basics: a crisp "why I am looking and why this role," a comp range tied to her floor, and a clean, non-negative account of her current or last role. Light on deep technical detail, heavy on fit and logistics. Prep two or three stories that prove the central requirement, and have one or two questions that show she read the job. End by asking what the next stage is and who is on it.

### Hiring-manager screen
Same shape, but substance starts here. The manager is asking "can she do this job and do I want her on my team." Lead with the stories closest to the core of the role, and be ready to talk tradeoffs she actually made, not just outcomes. Her questions should be about the work and the team, not process.

### Panel or sequential one-on-ones
Several interviewers, often back to back, sometimes with a shared rubric. The risk is inconsistency: a story stretched to impress one interviewer will not survive comparison across several, so keep every answer to the true version. Expect repeated questions across interviewers and answer each freshly rather than "as I told your colleague." Track energy across the block; a panel is a stamina test as much as a knowledge one.

### Onsite or final round
The longest, highest-cost look: an all-day schedule, possibly a presentation or work sample. Prep the logistics as much as the content: what to have in front of her, breaks, how to reset between sessions. If there is a work sample or presentation, that is the center of gravity; prepare it against the real job description. This is also when references are usually requested, so line them up in advance (`references/stage_guidance.md`, References).

## Use the story bank, and grow it

If `Story_Bank.md` is thin for this role, seed a story or two first (see `story_bank.md`). An interview is the best reason to add to the bank, and the new stories stay available for the next one.

## Guardrails

- Ground every suggested answer in something true from the profile or story bank. Never script an accomplishment she does not have.
- Prep her to answer honestly under pressure, including about gaps. A rehearsed true answer beats an improvised inflated one.
- Her voice. The prep should sound like her talking, not like a script she has to memorize.
- This is preparation, not a performance to recite. Keep it usable: a page she can review, not an essay.
