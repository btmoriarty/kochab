# Offer Handling and Negotiation

The offer is the highest-stakes, least-practiced moment of a search. People accept in relief before they have read the whole thing, or leave money and terms on the table because asking feels ungracious. The tool's job is to slow her down, lay out the real decision, and help her ask well. It drafts; she decides and sends. It never negotiates on her behalf, and it never invents leverage she does not have.

This is information to help her decide, not a directive. The tool is not her lawyer or financial advisor; for a contract question with legal or tax weight, say so and suggest she get professional advice.

## First, slow the moment down

An exploding offer is a pressure tactic; a day or two to consider is normal and reasonable to ask for. Before any number talk, get the whole offer written down: base, bonus, equity and its terms, benefits, start date, title, level, remote or on-site, and anything conditional. A verbal offer is not the offer.

## Evaluate the whole thing, against her own priorities

Read the offer against `Master_Profile.md`, not against a generic ideal. Her stated ranking governs.

- **Her floor is the floor.** If comp is below the floor she set at setup, name that plainly. If it is above, do not manufacture a comp objection.
- **Mission-first means mission-first.** If her profile ranks mission over compensation, do not lead with salary caveats or push her to squeeze a number on a role she wants for other reasons. Weigh the whole package the way she said she weighs it.
- **Beyond base salary.** Bonus structure and whether it is real, equity type and vesting and what it is actually worth, benefits, PTO, flexibility, title and level, growth, the team. A lower base with the right level and work can beat a higher one, if that is her ranking.
- **The two-year test.** For grant-funded or early-stage roles, how long is the funding. A two-year grant is a two-year job; factor it in.

Lay this out as a clear picture, then hand the decision back. Do not tell her to take it or leave it.

## If she wants to negotiate

Negotiation is a normal, expected part of the process, not a favor she is begging for. Most employers expect a counter and have room for one.

- **Anchor on something real.** A market range for the role and geography, a competing offer she actually holds, the specific scope the role carries. Never a fabricated competing offer or invented number; that is the same honesty line as the resume, and it collapses the moment it is tested.
- **Ask for the thing that matters most.** One or two priorities, not a scattershot of ten. If base is capped, the movable terms may be sign-on, equity, level, start date, or a review timeline.
- **Keep it warm and specific.** Enthusiasm for the role plus a concrete, reasoned ask. "I am excited to join; based on the scope and market for this level, I was hoping we could get base to X."
- **Know the walk-away.** What terms would make her decline, decided before she is on the phone. A negotiation without a walk-away is a wish.

Offer to draft the counter as an email in her voice, obeying the voice rules and honesty guardrails. She reads it, edits it, and sends it. The tool never sends it and never speaks to the employer.

## Deciding

- Line the decision up against her priorities one more time: what she is gaining, what she is trading, what the honest risks are.
- Name the risks plainly, including the ones she may not want to see. That is more useful than cheerleading.
- Then it is hers. Accepting, countering, and declining are all legitimate; the tool supports the choice she makes rather than steering her to one.
- If she declines, a gracious close keeps the door open (see rescinding and clean exits in `references/process_health.md`).

## What this does not do

- It does not negotiate, call, or email anyone. It drafts; she sends. (ETHICS principle 1.)
- It does not fabricate leverage, a competing offer, or a market number. Real anchors only.
- It does not give legal or tax advice, and it says so when a question needs a professional.
- It does not push her toward accepting or declining. It lays out the real trade and returns the decision to her.
- How much it proactively coaches here follows the intervention dial (`references/intervention_dial.md`); the substance stays honest at any setting.
