# Story Bank

A small set of true, specific, reusable stories from her career. Cover notes draw on it so they can be personal without being written from scratch each time; interview prep will draw on the same file later. It is seeded from the resume, then filled out through a short interview, and it grows as she applies.

The whole value of the bank is specificity. A generic story is worse than no story. One real anecdote with a real result beats three lines of "proven track record."

## Seeding from the resume

Read the resume. For every role or bullet that implies a real accomplishment, write a **stub**: a one-line pointer to a story that is probably in there. "Led the migration that took the outage rate down" is a stub. It is not a story yet; it is a candidate.

Present the stubs and let her pick the three to five with the most substance behind them. Do not try to build a story from every bullet; most bullets are not stories.

## The interview (this is where stubs become stories)

For each chosen stub, walk her through it as a conversation, one story at a time. Get to the concrete. The four beats:

- **Situation.** What was going on, and why did it matter? What was at stake if it went wrong?
- **Task.** What was hers to do, specifically? This is where you separate her part from the team's part.
- **Action.** What did she actually do? Push past "we" and "helped with" to the decisions and moves that were hers.
- **Result.** What changed? A number if there is one, a durable outcome if there is not. "Held it for three years" is a result. "Worked on improving things" is not.

Then the honesty pass, per story: **were you the owner of this, or adjacent to the owner?** Record the honest framing so no cover note ever inflates it. This is the same discipline as the profile's honesty guardrails, applied per anecdote.

Ask for the story in her own words first, then tighten it. Do not narrate it back in resume-speak; keep her phrasing where it is good.

## The file: `Story_Bank.md`

One entry per story:

```
### [Short tag, e.g. "The ransomware posture"]

**Demonstrates:** [the one or two capabilities this proves — e.g. "program leadership under regulatory pressure," "getting disconnected teams onto one standard"]

**Situation:** ...
**Task:** ...
**Action:** ...
**Result:** ...

**Honest framing:** [owner vs. adjacent; what not to overclaim about this one]
**Best for:** [the kind of role or requirement this story answers well]
```

Keep each story tight enough to reuse: a paragraph or two, not a page.

## Growing it over time

The bank does not have to be complete at setup. Three strong stories is enough to start. Offer to add one whenever she applies to something the current bank does not cover well, and whenever a scan surfaces a role that would be answered by an anecdote she has not written down yet.

When a story is used in an application, note where. Over time this shows which stories are carrying the search and which requirements keep coming up without a story to match, which is a useful signal in its own right.

## Guardrails

- Only true stories. Never invent an anecdote or a result to fill a gap.
- Keep the honest framing attached to every story, permanently. It travels with the story into every note.
- Numbers must be real. If she is not sure of a figure, record the range or the qualitative outcome, not a made-up precise number.
- Her voice, not resume-bot voice. The bank should read like her telling you what happened.
