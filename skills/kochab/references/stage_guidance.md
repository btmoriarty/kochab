# Stage Guidance

A search is a sequence of stages, and people lose ground because they do not know what a given stage is for, what it expects, or what comes after it. This reference is the map. It answers "I am at X, what now" for each stage of a process, and it covers references, the late-stage task most people handle clumsily.

It is keyed to the pipeline Stage vocabulary from `references/pipeline.md` (Applied, Screen, Interview, Final, Offer), so "where does this go next" lines up with what the tracker already records. Guidance is grounded in her profile, story bank, and the specific role, never generic. Prep for a specific interview at any of these stages is Interview mode (Step E, `references/interview_prep.md`), which this map points into; the map itself is the orientation around it.

## How to use the map

When she asks what a stage expects or what comes next, give her the entry for the stage she is at, plus a one-line look at the next one so she is not surprised. Keep it to what is useful now. Do not narrate all five stages when she asked about one.

Honesty holds here as everywhere: name a thin spot plainly and give her a true way to handle it, never a bluff. The guardrails from the profile apply to how she talks about her record at every stage.

## The phases

### Applied
- **Purpose:** the application is in; nothing is expected of her yet except the wait.
- **What it expects:** patience, and a follow-up at the right time (Momentum mode, `references/momentum.md`) if it goes quiet. Not a stage to over-manage.
- **How to be ready:** have the resume variant and note she sent saved, so a fast reply does not catch her flat-footed.
- **Next:** a recruiter or hiring-manager screen, or silence. If silence, the follow-up cadence covers it.

### Screen (recruiter or hiring-manager)
- **Purpose:** the cheapest filter in the process. It exists to cut, on avoidable things: unclear reason for looking, comp mismatch, a basic qualification gap, a red flag in how she talks about her last role.
- **What it expects:** a crisp answer to "why are you looking and why this role," a comp range she has thought about, and a clean, non-negative account of where she is coming from. A recruiter screen tests fit and logistics; a hiring-manager screen starts testing substance.
- **How to be ready:** prep it as an interview for the screen format (Step E). Have the one-line why-this-role, a comp range tied to her floor, and two or three stories that prove the central requirement. Know the thin spots and the honest handling of each.
- **Next:** a first or full interview round. Ask the recruiter what the next stage is and who is on it; they will usually tell you.

### Interview
- **Purpose:** to test whether she can actually do the work and how she thinks, in depth.
- **What it expects:** substance, not just fit. Worked examples, real tradeoffs she has made, how she handles the parts of the job the posting emphasizes.
- **How to be ready:** Interview mode, format-aware (Step E). Map likely questions to her real stories, prepare her own sharp questions, rehearse the thin spots honestly.
- **Next:** more rounds, a panel, or a final/onsite. Ask how many rounds remain so she can pace herself.

### Final (panel or onsite)
- **Purpose:** the last, highest-cost look, often several interviewers or a work sample. It tests depth, consistency across interviewers, and whether the team wants to work with her.
- **What it expects:** stamina and consistency. The same true stories hold up across a panel; a story stretched to impress one interviewer will not survive comparison across several.
- **How to be ready:** Step E, onsite/panel format: how to handle a battery of one-on-ones, an all-day schedule, a presentation or work sample. This is also when references usually get requested, so line them up now (below).
- **Next:** an offer, or a no. Either way the wait after a final is the hardest silence; the follow-up cadence applies.

### Offer
- **Purpose:** the decision moves to her.
- **What it expects:** a considered response, not an instant yes or a panicked accept.
- **How to be ready:** know her floor and what matters beyond salary before the number lands. Do not lead with caveats unless her profile ranks comp first.
- **Next:** negotiation and the accept/decline decision. **Offer handling and negotiation is a heavier, higher-stakes beat handled in v0.9**; at this stage the map's job is to slow her down, not to coach the negotiation. Hand off there.

## References

References are handled late and clumsily more than almost any other part of a search: asked in a panic the night before they are due, given no context, and thanked with nothing. Good reference handling is quiet leverage.

- **Who to ask.** People who genuinely observed her do the work: a former manager, a peer who partnered closely, a client or stakeholder who saw the result. Relevance to *this* role beats seniority; a director who barely knew her is worth less than a peer who did. Never a reference who would have to inflate to help; that violates the same honesty line as the resume.
- **When to ask.** Before they are needed, not when the form is in front of her. The moment a process reaches a final round is the signal to line them up, so the ask is "would you be willing, if it comes up" rather than "I need this by tomorrow."
- **How many.** Usually three, mixing a manager and a peer, and if the role is client-facing, someone she delivered for.
- **How to brief them.** Give each reference the role, the company, the two or three things this employer will care about, and the specific work of hers that speaks to them. A briefed reference tells a specific, useful story; an unbriefed one gives a warm blur. Brief them truthfully; never coach a reference on what to claim, only remind them what they saw.
- **How to close the loop.** Tell them how it went and thank them. References are a relationship, not a transaction, and she will need them again.

**Best and bad practices, short.**

- Do: ask early, choose for relevance, brief with the specifics, keep them warm, thank them after.
- Do not: surprise a reference with a call they did not expect, pick purely for title, hand them no context, coach them to overstate, or go silent afterward.

Reference handling is about her own professional relationships, distinct from network outreach for warm introductions, which is a bounded v1.0 feature drawing on a different data source. Keep the two separate.

## What this does not do

- It does not coach offer negotiation or the accept/decline decision (v0.9); it slows her down at the offer and hands off.
- It does not diagnose her funnel or read patterns across stages (v0.9).
- It does not run the interview prep itself; that is Step E. The map orients; Step E prepares.
- It never coaches a bluff, at any stage or in any reference. Honest and specific beats inflated and vague, every time.
